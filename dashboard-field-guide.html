🪜 Step by step

From Blank Page to Dashboard

Everything above is the menu. This is the order you actually work through it in, with a prompt for each step.
  1. Gather your data. Have it in Excel, a SharePoint library, or just described in chat — Copilot can read files in place or work from what you type.
  2. Pick your level. Foundations, Intermediate, or Expert — match it to how much time you have and how often the audience will interact with it.
  3. Pick your audience. A team-facing operating view, or an Executive/SLT readout — they need different amounts of detail.
  4. Pick your cadence. Daily, Weekly, Bi-weekly/Sprint, or Monthly — this decides which elements earn their place.
  5. Write one combined prompt. Stack the choices above into a single request rather than a vague one. See the example below.
  6. Review and refine one thing at a time. Don't ask for five changes in one message — ask for one, check it, then the next. Big multi-part edits are where generation quality drops.
  7. Export or share. Turn the result into a Copilot Page, or download the HTML/PowerPoint/Excel output depending on where it needs to live.
Prompt: "Build a weekly team-level dashboard: KPI cards with week-over-week trend arrows, a bar chart of output by owner, a slicer for owner, and a kanban board highlighting items due this week. Keep it to base and intermediate elements — no drill-downs."
Prompt: "Build a monthly executive dashboard: 4 KPI cards with sparklines and trend vs. target, a RAG status table by workstream, a budget gauge, and a one-line insight under each chart. Minimal low-saturation palette, full detail in a separate Details tab."
Rule of thumb: name the level, the audience, and the cadence explicitly in the prompt. Those three words do more work than any amount of extra styling detail.

🎨 Visual polish

Data Storytelling & Accessibility

The difference between a dashboard that informs and one that just displays.

Lead with the headline

Put the single most important number top-left — that's where the eye lands first. Everything else supports it.

One message per screen

If you can't summarize the point in one sentence, it's two dashboards, not one.

One metric per card

Resist cramming three numbers into one KPI card — split it into three cards instead.

Bold sparingly

Bold or color only the one number that matters. If everything is emphasized, nothing is.

Don't rely on color alone

Pair every RAG badge with an icon or label too (●On Track, ▲At Risk, ✕Off Track) — around 1 in 12 men have some form of color blindness.

Contrast & size

Aim for at least 4.5:1 text contrast, and keep body text no smaller than 14px — dashboards get projected and shrunk more than you'd expect.

Prompt: "Make sure every status badge pairs a color with an icon or text label, not color alone. Keep body text at least 14px and headline numbers high-contrast against the background."

📖 Glossary

Terms & Prompts Used in This Guide

Two lists: the short-form codes and abbreviations, and every copy-ready prompt from this guide gathered in one place.
📟 Codes & Abbreviations

KPI

Key Performance Indicator — a single tracked number, like revenue or task count.

RAG

Red / Amber / Green — a three-color status shorthand for at-a-glance health.

Slicer

A filter control (buttons, tabs, dropdown) that narrows what a view shows.

Sparkline

A tiny trend line inside a KPI card, with no axis labels.

Drill-down

Clicking through from a summary to more detail, e.g. company → team.

Cross-filtering

Clicking one chart automatically filters every other chart on the page.

Swimlane

A horizontal grouping on a kanban board, usually by owner or workstream.

WIP limit

Work-in-progress limit — a cap on how many items can sit in one kanban column at once.

SLT

Senior Leadership Team — shorthand for an executive audience.

Overlay

Any UI layer sitting above the main page — a modal, tooltip, or side drawer.

Cadence

How often a view is read and updated — daily, weekly, sprint, or monthly.

Burndown / Burnup chart

A chart showing work remaining (burndown) or completed (burnup) across a sprint.

Velocity

The average amount of work a team completes per sprint, used to forecast future sprints.

Status report

A periodic summary of a project's health, milestones, risks, and next steps.

Portfolio rollup

A single view listing multiple projects side by side, usually with a RAG badge each.

✅ Quality gate

Before You Hit Send: Review Checklist

Click to check off — nothing here is saved, it just helps you go through the list once before sharing.
  • Every number spot-checked against the source data
  • No slicer, tab, or filter that does nothing when clicked
  • Tested at a narrow (mobile) width, not just full screen
  • RAG status uses an icon or label, not color alone
  • One clear headline takeaway is visible in the first 3 seconds
  • Colors and fonts are consistent across every card and chart
  • No placeholder text, dummy links, or "lorem ipsum" left in
Self-critique prompt — paste this back into Copilot before sending: "Review this dashboard critically: check for visual clutter, inconsistent colors or fonts, unclear hierarchy, and confirm every filter actually works. Suggest 3 specific improvements."
📤 Last mile

Export & Handoff

Where the dashboard lives after you build it changes what you should ask for.

Keep as a Copilot Page

Best default — stays interactive (slicers, pop-ups, kanban drag-and-drop all keep working), and is easy to keep updating.

Export to Word

Good for a written status report; ask Copilot to "convert this into a Word document" — tables carry over cleanly, charts may need to become static images.

Export to PowerPoint

Best for the steering-committee deck template — ask for "one slide per section, with the KPI summary first."

Embed in SharePoint

Keeps interactivity alive for a team audience that returns to it regularly, unlike a flattened export.

PDF export

Fine for a one-off static leave-behind or an email attachment — but it flattens everything, so use it only for the final snapshot, never as your working copy.

Keep one source of truth

Treat the interactive version as the master copy, and generate flattened exports (Word/PPT/PDF) from it only when a specific audience needs a static file.

🛠 When It Goes Wrong

Quick Fixes for Bad Generations

You're staring at a broken or wrong result on screen. Here's what to do immediately, before you start over from scratch.

Blank page / nothing renders

Ask: "regenerate this as a single self-contained HTML file with no external dependencies or CDN links." Broken renders are usually a missing script reference, not a design problem.

Numbers don't match your data

Point at the source directly: "re-check every figure against [filename] — do not estimate or round, use the exact values." Vague prompts invite the model to fill gaps itself.

Categories or labels you never gave it

Say: "only use categories and labels present in the uploaded data — do not invent or infer new ones." This is the single most common cause of "wrong data pulled."

Chart doesn't update with new data

Re-upload or re-reference the file explicitly in the next message — don't assume it remembers a file from three prompts ago.

Colors clash or text is unreadable

Give exact hex codes instead of describing a vibe: "use #1B1D1A for text, #F2F1EC for background, one accent color only."

Layout breaks on mobile / table overflows

Ask for it explicitly: "make this responsive, wrap long text instead of overflowing, no fixed-width tables." (This exact fix is what repaired the reference table earlier in this guide.)

Kanban drag-and-drop doesn't work

Ask for "vanilla JavaScript drag-and-drop, no external libraries" — sandboxed environments often can't load outside packages.

Prompt only partly followed

You likely asked for too much in one message. Split it: get the base layout right first, then add slicers, then charts, then polish — one request per message.

General habit that fixes most of the above: after 2–3 failed patch attempts on the same issue, stop patching. Ask for a full, clean regeneration with a more specific prompt instead — small incremental fixes on a broken base tend to compound the problem rather than solve it.
Before you trust a number: spot-check at least two or three figures against the source yourself. AI-generated dashboards are convenient, not infallible — a five-second manual check catches most "confidently wrong" outputs.
