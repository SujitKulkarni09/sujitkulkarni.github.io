AWS in three sentences

AWS is Amazon renting out pieces of its own massive computing infrastructure — servers, storage, databases, networking — so companies don't have to buy and maintain their own. You pay only for what you use, and you can rent more the instant you need it, or less the instant you don't. Everything else on this page is really just the details of exactly what's available to rent, and when to reach for which piece.

01 · First principles

Why the cloud exists at all

Before AWS, if a company wanted a website, they bought physical servers, put them in a room, wired them up, and hoped they'd guessed the right amount of capacity.

Owning your own servers

Big upfront cost. You guess your capacity months in advance. Guess too low, the site crashes on your busiest day. Guess too high, you've wasted money on idle machines for years.

vs

Renting from AWS

Pay only for what you use, by the second or hour. Need 10x the capacity for Black Friday? Rent it for a day, then give it back. No guessing, no idle hardware.

This shift has a name: the shared responsibility model. AWS is responsible for the security of the cloud — the physical data centers, the hardware, the network backbone. You're responsible for security in the cloud — how you configure what you rent. Nearly every serious AWS mistake traces back to misunderstanding that line.

The trade you're actually making: you give up owning physical hardware, in exchange for speed, flexibility, and never again being the person who has to replace a failed hard drive at 3am. That trade is why AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud now underpin most of the software you use daily, even though you've never seen a single one of their servers.
02 · Orientation

Where AWS actually sits

Not a website host. Not one product. A rentable version of almost every piece of infrastructure a company would otherwise have to build itself.

In the journey from an idea to something people actually use:

Idea / Product→ Your Code
(what it does)→ AWS
(where it runs)→ The Internet→ You, the user

AWS doesn't write your application logic and it doesn't decide your product strategy — it provides the land, the electricity, and the plumbing your application runs on, at a scale no single company could build for itself.

AWS versus the other two major clouds:

ProviderIn plain English
AWS (Amazon)The largest and oldest major cloud, the broadest service catalog, the default choice at most companies
Azure (Microsoft)Strong where a company is already deep in Microsoft tools (Windows Server, Active Directory, Office 365)

You don't need to pick a side here — the core concepts (compute, storage, networking) transfer between all three almost one-to-one, just with different names.

🏠
Think of it likeA massive commercial landlord that doesn't just rent office space — it rents electricity, security guards, internet wiring, storage units, and even pre-built meeting rooms, all separately, all billed by the hour, to thousands of different companies out of the same building at once.
03 · In practice

One tap on your phone, traced through AWS

Same story format as before, this time focused purely on the AWS side. You open a food delivery app and tap "Order."

📱

You tap "Order." Your phone sends a request out over the internet, looking for the app's servers.

🌐

Route 53, AWS's DNS service, translates the app's address into the actual location of the nearest servers — the same way a phone book turns a name into a number.

⚖️

Your request lands on a Load Balancer, which picks one healthy server out of many to handle it, so no single machine gets overwhelmed.

🖥️

That server is an EC2 instance (or a Lambda function, or a container on ECS/EKS) — the actual rented compute running the app's code.

💾

To check your order, that code queries a database — RDS or DynamoDB — sitting in a private part of AWS's network your phone can never reach directly.

🔒

Every one of those pieces is locked down by IAM and Security Groups, so only the exact services that need to talk to each other can — nothing is open to the whole internet by accident.

📈

CloudWatch is quietly measuring how fast all of this happened, and CloudTrail is quietly logging exactly which AWS resource did what, in case anyone ever needs to ask why.

✅

Your order confirms. A dozen AWS services just cooperated, in well under a second, without you ever knowing any of their names — until right now.

🔌
Think of it likeElectricity. A hundred years ago, a factory that needed power had to build its own generator. Today, you just plug into the grid and pay for what you use. Your AWS Account, Billing & Free Tier
Where every mistake and every safeguard starts
▼
💳
Think of it likeA single tab at a restaurant that never asks you to pay upfront — every plate that leaves the kitchen gets added automatically, and the bill only closes out at the end of the month. It's incredibly convenient, and exactly why beginners get surprise charges: nothing stops you from ordering more than you meant to.
What the Free Tier actually covers

AWS's Free Tier gives limited usage of many services (like 750 hours/month of a small EC2 instance) free for 12 months on a new account, plus some services that are always free at low usage. It is not "everything free" — forgetting to shut down a bigger instance, or leaving an unused NAT Gateway running, is the single most common beginner billing surprise.

The one setting to configure on day one
terminalaws-cli
# set a budget alert so AWS emails you before spend gets out of hand
$ aws budgets create-budget --account-id 123456789012 \
  --budget file://budget.json --notifications-with-subscribers file://notify.json

A billing alarm costs nothing and takes five minutes — it is the single highest-value five minutes you'll spend as a beginner.

IAM Fundamentals
Users, Groups, Roles, and Policies — the security foundation for everything else
▼
🔑
Think of it likeA hotel's key-card system. A User is a person with a card. A Group is "all housekeeping staff" — give the group access once, everyone in it inherits it. A Role is a card that isn't tied to one person at all — a temporary card the maintenance robot picks up, uses, and drops off again. A Policy is the actual list of doors that card opens.
The rule almost every AWS breach traces back to

Over-permissioned access. The fix is the principle of least privilege: grant exactly the permissions needed, nothing more, written as an explicit JSON policy.

s3-read-only-policy.jsonjson
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [{
    "Effect": "Allow",
    "Action": ["s3:GetObject", "s3:ListBucket"],
    "Resource": ["arn:aws:s3:::app-uploads/*"]
  }]
}

GroupA collection of Users"All backend engineers get read access to logs"
RoleNobody permanently — assumed temporarilyAn EC2 instance or Lambda function accessing S3 without stored credentials

EC2 vs Lambda
The two most fundamental ways to run code on AWS
▼
🖥️
Think of it likeEC2 is renting a full apartment — you control everything inside it, and pay rent whether you're home or not. Lambda is calling a handyman who shows up the instant you need something fixed, does exactly that one task, and leaves — you only pay for the minutes they actually worked.
What each one actually is

EC2 runs a virtual machine you fully control — you choose the operating system, install anything, and it keeps running (and billing) until you stop it. Lambda runs your code only in response to a trigger (an API call, a file upload, a schedule), for at most 15 minutes, then shuts down completely — you're billed per millisecond of actual execution.

terminalaws-cli
# launch an EC2 instance
$ aws ec2 run-instances --image-id ami-0abc123 --instance-type t3.micro

# deploy and invoke a Lambda function
$ aws lambda create-function --function-name resize-image \
    --runtime nodejs20.x --handler index.handler --zip-file fileb://function.zip
When to reach for which
EC2Lambda
Best forLong-running apps, full OS control, predictable steady loadShort bursts of work, unpredictable traffic, event-driven tasks
BillingBy the hour/second, whether busy or idleBy the millisecond of actual execution — zero when idle
Max runtimeUnlimited — runs until you stop it15 minutes per invocation, hard limit
EC2 instance typesAMIsLambda triggerscold startsserverless

Containers on AWS: ECS vs EKS vs Fargate
Packaging your app once, running it consistently anywhere
▼
🚛
Think of it likeA shipping container that runs identically no matter which ship carries it. ECS is AWS's own dock crew managing those containers. EKS is the same idea, but run by the internationally standard Kubernetes crew instead of AWS's proprietary one. Fargate means you don't even manage the ship — AWS runs the containers for you, and you never touch the underlying server.
Three ways to run the same container

Choosing between them in practice
ecs-task-definition.json (excerpt)json
{
  "family": "api-server",
  "requiresCompatibilities": ["FARGATE"],
  "cpu": "256", "memory": "512",

}

Already running Kubernetes elsewhere, or need to stay portable across clouds? EKS. Fully committed to AWS and want the simplest path? ECS. Don't want to manage servers at all, on either? Add Fargate to either one.

S3 — Object Storage
The service almost every AWS architecture touches somewhere
▼
📁
Think of it likeAn infinitely large, infinitely reliable storage warehouse. You don't get a specific shelf — you just hand over a labeled box (a file) and a name (a key), and the warehouse guarantees it'll be exactly where you left it, no matter how many other boxes are stored there.
The core model: buckets and keys

Files ("objects") live inside a bucket, addressed by a unique key (essentially a path). There's no real folder structure underneath — folders you see in the console are just a visual grouping of keys that share a prefix.

$ aws s3 cp photo.jpg s3://my-app-uploads/users/42/photo.jpg
$ aws s3api put-bucket-versioning --bucket my-app-uploads \
    --versioning-configuration Status=Enabled

EBS vs EFS vs S3
Three kinds of storage that solve three different problems
▼
💾
Think of it likeEBS is a hard drive bolted into one specific computer — fast, but only that computer can use it. EFS is a shared network drive an entire office can open at once. S3 is a public storage locker facility — not "attached" to any one computer at all, reachable from anywhere over the internet.
Block vs File vs Object — the underlying difference
ServiceStorage typeAttached toTypical use
EBSBlock storageA single EC2 instanceA database's disk, an OS root volume
EFSFile storage (NFS)Many EC2 instances at onceShared config or media files across a fleet
S3Object storageNothing — accessed over HTTP from anywhereUser uploads, static website assets, backups
Why you can't just use S3 for everything

S3 doesn't support the low-level, byte-range file operations an operating system or a database engine needs to boot from or write to directly — it's built for storing and retrieving whole objects over a network, not acting as a live disk. That's the specific gap EBS fills.

RDS vs DynamoDB vs Aurora
Choosing where your application's data actually lives
▼
📊
Think of it likeRDS is a well-organized filing cabinet with strict labeled folders (tables, rows, columns) — great when your data has a clear, consistent shape. DynamoDB is a wall of labeled lockers — blazing fast to grab any one locker by its key, less suited to complex cross-referencing between lockers.
SQL vs NoSQL, and why the choice matters early

RDS is a managed relational (SQL) database — AWS handles patching, backups, and failover for engines like PostgreSQL, MySQL, and SQL Server, but the data model is still rigid tables with relationships. DynamoDB is a managed NoSQL key-value/document store, built for near-instant reads and writes at massive scale, with a data model you design around your access patterns up front, not normalized relationships.

terminalaws-cli
# RDS: structured, relational
$ aws rds create-db-instance --db-instance-identifier orders-db \
    --engine postgres --db-instance-class db.t3.micro

# DynamoDB: key-value, schema-less beyond the primary key
$ aws dynamodb create-table --table-name Orders \
    --attribute-definitions AttributeName=OrderId,AttributeType=S \
    --key-schema AttributeName=OrderId,KeyType=HASH \
    --billing-mode PAY_PER_REQUEST
Where Aurora fits in
OptionWhat it isBest for
RDSManaged standard database engines (Postgres, MySQL, etc.)Familiar SQL, moderate scale, easy migration from on-prem
AuroraAWS's own MySQL/Postgres-compatible engine, re-architected for the cloudSame SQL familiarity, but higher throughput and faster failover than stock RDS
DynamoDBFully managed NoSQL, no servers to size at allMassive scale, single-digit-millisecond latency, simple access patterns

Think of it likeRenting an entire private office floor inside a shared building. You decide the internal layout — which rooms (subnets) exist, which have a door to the street (public) and which are internal-only (private) — even though the building itself is shared with thousands of other tenants.

Security Groups vs NACLs
Two firewalls, working at two different layers
▼
🛡️
Think of it likeA Security Group is a bouncer standing at one specific apartment door, checking every single person who tries to enter or leave that one unit. A NACL is the security desk at the building's main entrance, applying blanket rules to everyone entering or leaving the whole floor.
The key behavioral difference: stateful vs stateless
Security GroupNACL
Applies toIndividual resources (an EC2 instance, RDS database)An entire subnet
Rule typeAllow rules onlyAllow and explicit deny rules
Stateful?Yes — if inbound is allowed, the response is automatically allowed outNo — inbound and outbound rules must both be written explicitly
A Security Group, as code
main.tfterraform
resource "aws_security_group" "web" {
  ingress {
    from_port = 443
    to_port   = 443
    protocol  = "tcp"
    cidr_blocks = ["0.0.0.0/0"]
  }
}

Most real architectures use both: a NACL as a broad outer wall around the subnet, and a Security Group as the precise inner lock on each resource.

Load Balancing & Auto Scaling
Handling traffic that isn't the same size every hour
▼
🏃
Think of it likeA restaurant host directing guests to whichever open table is ready (Load Balancer), while the manager calls in extra waitstaff the moment the dinner rush starts, and sends them home once it's quiet (Auto Scaling).
How they work together

An Elastic Load Balancer (ELB) distributes incoming requests across multiple EC2 instances, so no single one is overwhelmed and unhealthy instances are automatically skipped. An Auto Scaling Group watches metrics like CPU usage and automatically launches new instances under load, then terminates them once demand drops — and the load balancer picks them up the moment they're healthy.

terminalaws-cli
$ aws autoscaling put-scaling-policy --auto-scaling-group-name web-fleet \
    --policy-name scale-on-cpu --policy-type TargetTrackingScaling \
    --target-tracking-configuration file://cpu-target.json
# target: keep average CPU across the fleet near 60%
Why this is what makes "elastic" real, not marketing

This pairing is the literal mechanism behind AWS's core promise from stage 01: capacity that grows and shrinks automatically with real demand, instead of a fixed number of servers sized for your worst-case guess.

Application Load BalancerAuto Scaling Groupshealth checkstarget tracking scaling

Route 53 & CloudFront
DNS and content delivery — getting users to the right place, fast
▼
📧
Think of it likeRoute 53 is the phone book that turns a business's name into its actual address. CloudFront is a chain of local mini-warehouses that pre-stock a store's popular items near every customer, so nobody has to wait on a shipment from the main warehouse across the country.
Two different jobs, often used together
ServiceJob
Route 53DNS — translates a domain name into an IP address, and can route based on latency, health, or geography
CloudFrontCDN — caches content at Edge Locations (stage 02) close to users, so it doesn't have to travel from the origin every time
terminalaws-cli
$ aws route53 change-resource-record-sets --hosted-zone-id Z123 \
    --change-batch file://record.json  # points app.example.com at your load balancer
Why this measurably speeds up a real user's experience

Without CloudFront, every user's request for a static image travels all the way to your origin Region. With it, a user in Singapore gets that same image from a nearby Edge Location — often the difference between a page loading instantly and visibly stalling.

CloudWatch vs CloudTrail vs X-Ray
Three different questions, three different tools
▼
🔍
Think of it likeA building's security system. CloudWatch is the thermostat and motion sensors — is everything running normally right now? CloudTrail is the sign-in logbook at the front desk — who came in, and when? X-Ray is the security footage that follows one specific visitor's exact path through every room.
Three questions, three tools
ToolAnswers
CloudWatch"Is something wrong, and how bad?" — metrics, logs, and alarms on your resources
CloudTrail"Who did what, and when?" — an audit log of every API call made in your account
X-Ray"Where exactly did a single request slow down?" — distributed tracing across services
terminalaws-cli
$ aws cloudwatch put-metric-alarm --alarm-name high-cpu \
    --metric-name CPUUtilization --threshold 80 --comparison-operator GreaterThanThreshold

$ aws cloudtrail lookup-events --lookup-attributes AttributeKey=Username,AttributeValue=jdoe
Why you eventually need all three, not just one

CloudWatch tells you a checkout API is slow. X-Ray shows the slowness is actually in a single DynamoDB call inside it. CloudTrail confirms a permissions change was deployed ten minutes before the slowdown started. Together, they turn "something's wrong" into a specific, provable root cause.

SNS vs SQS vs EventBridge
Letting AWS services talk to each other without being tightly coupled
▼
📣
Think of it likeSNS is a school's PA system — one announcement, heard by every classroom subscribed to it at once. SQS is a single ticket queue at a counter — each ticket gets handled by exactly one clerk, once. EventBridge is a smart mailroom that reads the label on each package and routes it to a completely different department based on what it says.
Broadcast vs queue vs smart routing
ServicePatternBest for
SNSPublish once, deliver to many subscribers (pub/sub)"Notify every downstream service the moment an order is placed"
SQSOne message, consumed by exactly one workerReliable task queues that must not be dropped, even under load
EventBridgeRule-based routing of events from many sources to many targetsConnecting dozens of services and SaaS tools by event type, not hardcoded destinations
terminalaws-cli
$ aws sns publish --topic-arn arn:aws:sns:...:order-placed --message "order #4521"
$ aws sqs send-message --queue-url https://sqs.../order-queue --message-body "order #4521"

A common real pattern: SNS broadcasts "order placed" to several SQS queues at once (fan-out) — one queue for email confirmation, one for inventory update, one for analytics — each processed independently, so a failure in one doesn't block the others.

The Well-Architected Framework
AWS's own checklist for whether an architecture is actually good
▼
📋
Think of it likeA building inspector's checklist, but for cloud architecture — six categories an inspector walks through before signing off that a design is actually sound, not just "it works on the demo."
The six pillars
PillarThe question it asks
Operational ExcellenceCan you run and monitor this system, and improve it over time?
SecurityIs access locked down to least privilege, and is data protected in transit and at rest?
ReliabilityDoes it recover automatically from failure, and survive an AZ outage?
Performance EfficiencyAre you using the right resource types for the workload, not just the familiar ones?
Cost OptimizationAre you paying for capacity you actually need?

Multi-Account Strategy & AWS Organizations
Why large companies never run everything in one account
▼
🏢
Think of it likeA large company giving each department its own separate office floor with its own locked door, instead of everyone sharing one giant open room — a mistake in Marketing's space can never accidentally lock Finance out of theirs.
Why one account isn't enough at scale

A single AWS account mixes billing, permissions, and blast radius for everything in it — a misconfigured IAM policy in a test project can theoretically affect production. AWS Organizations lets a company manage many accounts (dev, staging, prod, per-team) under one umbrella, with centralized billing and policy guardrails applied consistently across all of them.

ConceptWhat it does
AWS OrganizationsGroups multiple accounts, consolidates billing
Service Control Policy (SCP)An organization-wide guardrail — e.g. "no account may ever create a public S3 bucket"
Landing ZoneA pre-built, secure multi-account structure new accounts are provisioned into automatically
Why this is the norm at any real company

Separate accounts per environment mean a mistake in a developer's sandbox account is physically incapable of touching production — not just prevented by a permission, but by an entirely separate account boundary. This is table stakes at any company past a handful of engineers.

05 · Corrections

What AWS is not

Myth

You have to master all 200+ AWS services to get a job.

Reality

Most working roles use a core set of 15–20 services deeply, not the entire catalog broadly. This page's 23 stages already cover what the vast majority of real jobs actually touch.

Myth

"The cloud" is always cheaper than owning your own servers.

Reality

At very large, steady, predictable scale, owning hardware can be cheaper. AWS wins on flexibility and avoiding upfront cost, not on being universally the lowest price.

Myth

If something breaks, it's AWS's fault.

Reality

The shared responsibility model (stage 01) means most real-world incidents trace back to a customer misconfiguration — an open S3 bucket, an over-permissioned IAM role — not an AWS outage.

Myth

Serverless (Lambda) means there are no servers at all.

Reality

There are absolutely still servers — "serverless" means you never have to think about, provision, or patch them yourself.

06 · Common questions

Frequently asked, honestly answered

Do I need to know how to code to learn AWS?
To understand the concepts on this page, no. To actually build with AWS day-to-day, eventually yes — most real work involves the CLI, Infrastructure as Code, or SDKs, not just clicking through the console.
Is AWS free to learn?
Mostly, yes. The Free Tier (stage 03) covers enough usage to practice almost everything on this page without spending money, as long as you remember to shut resources down and set a billing alarm.
Should I start with AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud?
AWS has the largest market share and the most learning resources, which makes it a safe default starting point. The core concepts (compute, storage, networking, IAM) transfer to the other two almost one-to-one once you understand them deeply in one.
Do I need a certification to get hired?
No, but it helps signal baseline knowledge, especially with no prior professional experience. A demonstrated project (even a small one, built and deployed yourself) usually carries more weight than the certification alone.
What's the very first thing I should actually do, hands-on?
Create a free-tier account, set a billing alarm (stage 03), create an IAM user instead of using the root account (stage 04), and launch one EC2 instance. That single loop teaches more than reading about ten more services.
What happens if I never learn any of this?
Nothing, if you're not planning to build software yourself — you'll keep benefiting from it invisibly, the way the "Day in the Life" story above describes. This page exists so the words stop being a wall of jargon the next time you hear them.
07 · Reference

Glossary

ARN
Amazon Resource Name — the unique identifier AWS gives every single resource, used in IAM policies to say exactly what a rule applies to.
AMI
Amazon Machine Image — a saved template (OS + installed software) used to launch new EC2 instances.
Elasticity
The ability of a system to automatically grow or shrink its resources to match real demand.
Idempotent
Running the same operation multiple times has the same effect as running it once — important for infrastructure-as-code tools that re-apply configuration safely.
Managed service
A service where AWS handles the underlying infrastructure, patching, and scaling, so you only manage configuration and data.
Endpoint
The specific network address used to reach an AWS service or a deployed model/API.
Provisioning
The act of creating and configuring a resource (a server, a database) so it's ready to use.
Blast radius
How much of a system is affected if a given resource, account, or change fails or is compromised.
